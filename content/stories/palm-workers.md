## The question behind the diaries

A new discussion paper follows the money in both directions at once. Using high-frequency financial diaries collected from 29 Fijian temporary migrant workers in Australia and their families back home, it tracks how remittances respond when a worker's earnings rise and fall from one fortnight to the next. Financial diaries are a well-established way to see the cash flows that surveys miss, in the tradition of *Portfolios of the Poor*. Here the workers in Australia and the recipient households in Fiji were tracked weekly, in parallel, for up to a year, yielding roughly 550 fortnightly observations on each side.

## Workers save nearly half of what they earn

The averages from 2022 to 2023 cut against a common worry that workers take home almost nothing. A PALM worker earned about A$1,943 per fortnight after tax, sent A$459 home, spent A$566 on living costs in Australia, and saved A$919, close to half of their pay. On the receiving end, Fijian households took in about FJD 633 per fortnight, near A$400, and that flow made up roughly 90% of their income. Those households saved only about FJD 189, leaving little cushion for a bad week.

## Remittances are sticky, not proportional

Higher earnings do not translate into proportionally larger transfers. Workers who earn more in a given fortnight tend to save the surplus rather than send noticeably more, keeping the regular transfer fixed at the level the household needs. The pattern echoes Engel's Law: the remittance share of income falls as income rises. A 1% increase in earnings is associated with only about a 0.3% increase in remittances in the same fortnight. Because PALM workers face fixed hourly rates and employer-determined rosters, shifts, overtime, and shutdowns, the fortnight-to-fortnight swings in their pay are plausibly exogenous, which is what lets the study read these responses as causal.

## Bad weeks hit home, good weeks do not

Tracking both sides at once reveals an asymmetry that a one-sided study would miss. The marginal propensity to consume remittance income in Fiji is around 0.7: each dollar that the income flow moves shifts spending by about 70 cents. Fijian households respond more sharply to fluctuations than the migrants themselves, because the workers hold a savings buffer and the households largely do not. When a worker has a lean fortnight, the shortfall passes straight through to the family; when a worker has a good fortnight, the extra is absorbed into savings or personal spending rather than sent on. There is no evidence of consumption smoothing in Fiji.

## Why protecting the floor matters

The implication runs from the abattoir floor in regional Australia to the kitchen table in Fiji. Because remittance-dependent households hold so little buffer, volatility in a worker's Australian earnings becomes volatility in a family's consumption back home. The minimum hours, pay, and deduction protections now in place for PALM workers may therefore matter as much for the households receiving remittances as for the workers earning them. Protecting the floor of PALM workers' earnings may, in effect, protect the consumption floor in the countries they support.
